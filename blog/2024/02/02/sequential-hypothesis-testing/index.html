We introduce the mSPRT and give a derivation from a Bayesian point of view.

Sequential Testing

Normally when you go about hypothesis testing, after a random sample is observed one of two possible actions are taken: accept the null hypothesis H0H_0H0​, or accepct the tive hypothesis H1H_1H1​. In some cases the evidence may strongly support one of the hypotheses, whilst in other cases the evidence may be less convincing. Nevertheless, a decision must he made. All this assumes that all the data has been collected, and no more is available. It doesn’t have to be this way. There is a class of hypothesis tests where you can safely collect more data when the evidence is ambiguous. Such a test typically continues until the evidence strongly favors one of the two hypotheses.

In case of simple hypotheses the strength of the evidence for HHH is given by the ratio of the probability of the data under H0H_0H0​, to the probability of the data under H1H_1H1​. We denote this likelihood ratio by Λ\LambdaΛ. The Neyman-Pearson lemma implies that for a given amount of information the likelihood ratio test is the most powerful test. Such a rule decides to accept H1H_1H1​ if Λ\LambdaΛ is big enough, and decides to accept H0H_0H0​ otherwise. How big Λ\LambdaΛ must get to lead to the decicion H1H_1H1​, danends on, amond other things, its sampling distribution under H0H_0H0​ and H1H_1H1​. It is not unusual for “big enough” to mean Λ≥1\Lambda \geq 1Λ≥1. Such tests could easily decide H0H_0H0​ or H1H_1H1​ when the actual evidence is neutral.

SPRT

In 1943 Wald proposed the sequential probability ratio test (SPRT). Suppose that YYY is a random variable with unknown distribution fff. We want to test the following hypotheses:

  • H0:f=f0H_0: f=f_0H0​:f=f0​
  • H1:f=f1H_1: f=f_1H1​:f=f1​

where f0f_0f0​ and f1f_1f1​ are specified. We observe values of YYY successively: y1,y2,…y_1, y_2, \ldotsy1​,y2​,… the random variables YiY_iYi​ corresponding to the yiy_iyi​ are i.i.d with common distribution fff. Let

Λ=∏i=1nf1(xi)f0(xi)\Lambda = \prod_{i=1}^n \frac{f_1\left(x_i\right)}{f_0\left(x_i\right)}Λ=i=1∏n​f0​(xi​)f1​(xi​)​

be the likelihood ratio at stage nnn. We choose two decision boundaries AAA and BBB such that 0<B<A<∞0 < B < A < \infty0<B<A<∞, we accept H0H_0H0​ if Λ≤B\Lambda \leq BΛ≤B and H1H_1H1​ if Λ≥A\Lambda \geq AΛ≥A, and we continue if B≤Λ≤AB \leq \Lambda \leq AB≤Λ≤A. The constants AAA and BBB are determined by the desired false positive and false negative rates of the experimenter. In general it can be shown that the boundaries A and B can be calculated as with very good approximation as

A=log⁡(β1=α)A=\log\left(\frac{\beta}{1=\alpha}\right)A=log(1=αβ​) B=log⁡(1−βα)B=\log\left(\frac{1-\beta}{\alpha}\right)B=log(α1−β​)

so the SPRT is really very simple to apply in practice.

The Mixture Sequential Probability Ratio Test (mSPRT)

The main limitation of the Sequential Probability Ratio Test (SPRT) is its requirement for specifying an explicit alternative hypothesis, which might not always align with the goal of merely rejecting a null hypothesis. In contrast, the modified Sequential Probability Ratio Test (mSPRT) offers flexibility in determining sample sizes at the start of an experiment, unlike the traditional SPRT, where sample size calculations are not feasible. Consequently, parameters such as NNN, α\alphaα, and β\betaβ are fixed at the outset of an experiment.

The mixture Sequential Probability Ratio Test (mSPRT) amends these limitations. The test is defined by a “mixing” distribution HHH over a parameter space Θ\ThetaΘ, where HHH is assumed to have a density hhh that is positive everywhere. Utilizing HHH, we first compute the following mixture of likelihood ratios against the null hypothesis θ=θ0\theta = \theta_0θ=θ0​:

Λn(sn)=∫Θ(fθ(sn)fθ0(sn))ndH(θ).\Lambda_n(s_n) = \int_{\Theta} \left(\frac{f_{\theta}(s_n)}{f_{\theta_0}(s_n)}\right)^n dH(\theta).Λn​(sn​)=∫Θ​(fθ0​​(sn​)fθ​(sn​)​)ndH(θ).

The mSPRT is parameterized by a mixing distribution HHH over Θ\ThetaΘ, which is restricted to have an everywhere continuous and positive derivative. Given an observed sample average sns_nsn​ up to time nnn, the likelihood ratio of θ\thetaθ against θ0\theta_0θ0​ is (fθ(sn)fθ0(sn))n\left(\frac{f_{\theta}(s_n)}{f_{\theta_0}(s_n)}\right)^n(fθ0​​(sn​)fθ​(sn​)​)n. Thus, we define the mixture likelihood ratio with respect to HHH as:

Λn(sn)=∫Θ(fθ(sn)fθ0(sn))ndH(θ).\Lambda_n(s_n) = \int_{\Theta} \left(\frac{f_{\theta}(s_n)}{f_{\theta_0}(s_n)}\right)^n dH(\theta).Λn​(sn​)=∫Θ​(fθ0​​(sn​)fθ​(sn​)​)ndH(θ).

Application to Normal Data

Considering normal data, for any μA\mu_AμA​ and μB\mu_BμB​, the difference Zn=Yn−XnZ_n = Y_n - X_nZn​=Yn​−Xn​ follows a normal distribution ∼N(θ,2σ2)\sim N(\theta, 2\sigma^2)∼N(θ,2σ2). We can apply the one-variation mSPRT to the sequence {Zn}\{Z_n\}{Zn​}, leading to the following definition:

Here’s a small simulation:

def compute_mSPRT(y_data, x_data, sigma_squared, tau_squared, theta_0):
    n = len(y_data)
    y_mean = np.mean(y_data)
    x_mean = np.mean(x_data)
    lambda_n = np.sqrt(2 * sigma_squared / (2 * sigma_squared + n * tau_squared)) * \
               np.exp(n * n * tau_squared * (y_mean - x_mean - theta_0) ** 2 / (4 * sigma_squared * (2 * sigma_squared + n * tau_squared)))
    return lambda_n
